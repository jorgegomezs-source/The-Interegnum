Self-Consciousness as Organizational Capacity: From Real Movement to Revolutionary Practice

A Hegelian-Marxist Framework for Transformation in the Interregnum

By The Interregnum Reading time: 15 min

This article reconceptualizes revolutionary self-consciousness as collective organizational capacity—the practical know-how developed through material struggle—rather than individual ideological correctness. Arguing that we occupy an "interregnum" between decaying capitalist orders and emergent alternatives, it diagnoses how alienation paradoxically generates the technical capabilities needed to transcend capitalism itself.

Self-Consciousness as Organizational Capacity: From Real Movement to Revolutionary Practice

A Hegelian-Marxist Framework for Transformation in the Interregnum

Executive Summary

This article argues that communism must be understood not as a theoretical ideal but as the real movement that abolishes the present state of things (Marx & Engels, 1845–1846). At its core is a reconceptualization of self-consciousness as collective organizational capacity—the know-how developed through material struggle under capitalism, which contains within it the potential to transcend capitalism itself. Analyzing contemporary conditions through this framework reveals that we are living in an interregnum—a transitional period between decaying capitalist orders and emergent alternatives. The central political question today is whether the capabilities developed through alienation, crisis, and defensive adaptation can achieve organizational take-off: the shift from surviving within capitalism to building the social relations that abolish it. Through case studies, scenario analysis, and concrete pathways, this article provides a diagnostic and strategic toolkit for transformative practice.

I. Introduction: Communism as Real Movement

Self-consciousness is collective capacity, not individual lucidity. Following Marx and Engels in The German Ideology, we understand that communism is not a premeditated utopia to be established, an ideal to which reality must conform, but rather the real movement which abolishes the present state of things (Marx & Engels, 1845–1846). The conditions of this movement result from the premises now in existence: the material conditions of class struggle and capitalist development that simultaneously generate exploitation and the means of its transcendence.

Transformative social change emerges not from ideological conversion but from the organizational take-off of capabilities developed through struggle. The interregnum—the extended crisis between systemic orders (Wallerstein, 2004)—generates defensive adaptations that develop technical knowledge, institutional experience, and collective memory exceeding their current organization. When these capabilities achieve collective self-consciousness through practical coordination around immediate needs, they become the foundation for abolishing rather than merely reforming existing relations.

The argument proceeds through five analytic moves. First, a Hegelian-Marxist reconceptualization of consciousness as practical achievement emerging from material conditions rather than introspective awareness. Second, an analysis of alienation as the objective development of capabilities that transcend their alienated form. Third, a diagnosis of post-Fordist domination and its distinctive contradictions. Fourth, an examination of the mechanism by which developed capabilities achieve organizational take-off as real movement. Fifth, concrete pathways for translating the abolition of the present state into practice.

Glossary: Interregnum – The extended transitional period between the decomposition of one dominant world-system and the consolidation of another, characterized by systemic instability, ideological flux, and emergent organizational experiments (Wallerstein, 2004).

II. From Ideology to Real Movement

The Cartesian tradition treats self-consciousness as individual introspection, the cogito grounding certainty in self-transparent reflection. Against this, the Hegelian-Marxist tradition understands consciousness as practical achievement developed through recognition and labor (Hegel, 1807; Marx, 1845). Marx's critique of idealism in The German Ideology is decisive: "the production of ideas, of conceptions, of consciousness, is at first directly interwoven with the material activity and the material intercourse of men" (Marx & Engels, 1845–1846). Consciousness does not precede practice but emerges from it.

This has profound implications for understanding transformation. The question is not whether individuals have achieved correct consciousness, but whether the practices in which they are engaged develop capabilities that exceed their current organization. Communism is not an abstract program to be implemented but the practical activity through which the proletariat abolishes the conditions of its own existence as a class (Marx, 1845). Self-consciousness, in the full sense, is collective capacity—the organizational know-how that enables coordinated action toward the abolition of existing social relations.

Carlos Pérez Soto's formulation distinguishes consciousness as mind from consciousness as acts (Pérez Soto, 2013). Workers may hold conservative views while engaging in practices that develop transformative capabilities; radical beliefs may coexist with practices that reproduce existing relations. Unlike Althusserian models that treat ideology as all-encompassing, or orthodox Leninist approaches that assume correct consciousness must be brought from outside, Pérez Soto's framework recognizes the immanent development of transformative capacity through practice itself. The gap between empirical consciousness and class consciousness reflects the genuine contradiction between the consciousness guiding action and the consciousness action develops.

III. Alienation and the Development of Abolition

Alienation is an objective condition, not merely subjective experience. The worker in a capitalist enterprise need not feel alienated to be alienated. The organization of production itself—separation from means of production, subordination to managerial authority—constitutes alienation regardless of subjective state (Marx, 1844). Yet this alienation develops capabilities exceeding its own logic, capabilities that become the material basis for abolishing the conditions that produced them.

The factory system that alienates workers from their product develops their technical skills, coordination capacity, and production knowledge. These capabilities emerge in alienated form—knowledge belonging to capital, coordination serving profit. But their material existence—trained workers, operational facilities, proven solutions—constitutes developed powers that could be organized differently. The premises for abolishing the present state of things are generated by the present state itself.

This analysis extends to social reproduction—the often-invisibilized labor of care, socialization, and daily maintenance that sustains life and labor power. Feminist political economy (Federici, 2012; Fraser, 2016) reveals how capitalism simultaneously exploits and destabilizes social reproduction, generating crises of care that force communities to develop alternative coordination mechanisms. These crisis-generated capabilities—from mutual aid networks to community kitchens—represent another dimension of alienated yet potentially liberatory capacity.

Revolutionary practice operates with partial, incomplete consciousness of its own realities. This is not a defect to be overcome but the necessary form of transformative practice. The question is whether capabilities developed through alienated practice can achieve organizational take-off through transformation of the social relations organizing their deployment. The movement does not proceed from an ideal blueprint but from the practical necessity of addressing conditions that have become intolerable.

Case Study: The Rojava Cooperative Network

Case Study: The Rojava Cooperative Network (2026 Update)

The transformation of wartime logistics into cooperative infrastructure in northern Syria illustrates how developed capabilities achieve organizational take-off, even as the political structures that birthed them undergo fundamental transition. During the conflict against ISIS, the Autonomous Administration of North and East Syria (DAANES) developed decentralized supply networks to circumvent blockades and coordinate military-civilian production. These networks emerged not from a prior ideological blueprint but from the material necessity of survival under siege.

When military necessity diminished, these capabilities did not dissolve. The procedural knowledge of decentralized coordination and the technical infrastructure of alternative supply chains persisted as an "organizational inheritance." Between 2019 and 2024, approximately 4,200 agricultural and industrial cooperatives emerged, repurposing coordination mechanisms once used for military logistics toward civilian production.

By January 2026, the "Rojava experiment" entered a new phase following a major offensive by the Syrian transitional government. Under the comprehensive integration agreement of January 30, 2026, DAANES institutions began folding into central state bodies. While the era of de facto autonomy has effectively ended, the legacy of its cooperative networks faces a critical test of endurance. The agreement specifies that civilian employees will retain their posts, raising the possibility that the technical achievements of the cooperative system may survive as specialized local administrative units within the unified Syrian framework.

The transformation was never automatic. External pressures and internal contradictions always limited the scope of development. Yet the case demonstrates a vital mechanism: capabilities developed in one context—wartime survival—become resources for a different social organization when the original deployment transforms. As these networks move from autonomous revolutionary tools to integrated state-administered cooperatives, the "real movement" continues to proceed from existing material conditions rather than ideal plans.

Additional Case: Platform Cooperativism and Digital Capacity

The digital realm represents a contemporary frontier of both alienation and organizational potential. Platform capitalism extracts value through data surveillance and algorithmic control while developing unprecedented capacities for coordination, communication, and knowledge-sharing. Platform cooperativism—exemplified by:

  • The Drivers Cooperative, a driver-owned ride-hailing app in NYC that returns 100% of profits to workers, countering the extraction of the gig economy.
  • Stocksy United, a photographer-owned agency that pays artists up to 75% of sales, ensuring creators retain control over their digital labor and intellectual property.
  • Fairbnb.coop, a hospitality platform that reinvests half of its fees into local community projects to mitigate the negative social impacts of tourism.
  • CoopCycle, an international federation of delivery co-ops that uses a "Commons-based" license to ensure its software is only used by worker-owned entities.
  • Up & Go, a digital marketplace where professional cleaners retain 95% of their earnings and hold equal voting rights in business decisions.

These efforts demonstrate that digital infrastructure, though developed under capitalist relations, contains the technical and social protocols for alternative coordination. Similarly, open-source ecosystems and digital commons (like Wikipedia or Linux) embody practical knowledge of peer production that prefigures post-capitalist social relations.

IV. New Forms of Domination and Their Contradictions

Post-Fordist capitalism operates through repressive tolerance: the system's efficacy marginalizes direct repression while tolerance itself becomes the primary vehicle of subjection (Marcuse, 1965). New work organizations have reduced classical components of physical fatigue and psychological components of vertical, compulsive, direct domination. This is exploitation without oppression—exploitation that does not generate visible suffering prompting resistance.

Glossary: Repressive Tolerance – A form of social control where dissent is permitted and even encouraged within boundaries that ultimately reinforce systemic domination, creating the appearance of freedom while neutralizing transformative potential.

The frustrating agreeableness of these conditions generates dissatisfaction widely observed but difficult to conceptualize politically. Workers are neither the exploited proletariat of classical Marxism nor satisfied consumers of bourgeois ideology. They are subjects whose developed capabilities exceed the social relations organizing them, but who lack organizational forms for transformative deployment. The material conditions for abolishing these relations develop even as the political forms for doing so remain unclear.

Bureaucratic hegemony increasingly displaces bourgeois hegemony. The highly technological bureaucracy achieves control through administrative rationality, technical expertise, and organizational procedure (Wright, 2010). The same planning capacity that could organize production for social need currently organizes it for profit and control. The contradiction for transformative practice is navigating capabilities developed under bureaucratic capitalism toward the abolition of the conditions that produced them.

V. The Real Movement of Organizational Take-Off

Organizational take-off refers to the transformation from defensive adaptation to proactive abolition of existing conditions. The interregnum generates numerous developed capabilities: sanctions-evasion mechanisms, climate adaptation infrastructure, digital sovereignty expertise, care economy formations. These capabilities are currently misdirected toward defensive adaptation within capitalist relations. Their material existence constitutes achieved possibility for abolishing rather than merely escaping those relations.

Glossary: Organizational Take-off – The threshold moment when capabilities developed through struggle achieve sufficient coordination, scale, and self-awareness to begin transforming the social relations that originally produced them, moving from resistance within the system to building alternatives beyond it.

The decisive factor is organizational development emerging from practical necessity, not consciousness-raising as traditionally conceived. The gap between empirical consciousness and class consciousness bridges through practical coordination around immediate needs. When climate disaster creates care needs creates employment needs, articulating these crises through concrete organizational forms develops the procedural knowledge enabling different coordination. The movement proceeds from premises now in existence.

The mechanism is immanent to the contradiction. The same frustrating agreeableness stabilizing post-Fordist exploitation develops technical knowledge, coordination experience, and collective problem-solving capacity that could be organized differently. The debt financing extraction could become public ownership. The productivity gains captured as profit could become reduced labor time. These are determinate possibilities developed from existing achievements, not utopian projections.

Counter-Strategies and the Defense of Movement: Historical and Contemporary Examples

Ruling classes and bureaucracies deploy predictable counter-strategies against organizational take-off, often perfected through historical repetition:

  1. Co-optation absorbs transformative initiatives into existing structures. Historical examples include the institutionalization of radical labor unions into collective bargaining systems that manage rather than challenge capital (Arrighi, 1994). Contemporary examples include "social entrepreneurship" frameworks that convert community initiatives into profit centers, or NGOization that professionalizes and depoliticizes social movements (Choudry & Kapoor, 2013).
  2. Preemption channels emergent capabilities before they achieve critical mass. Corporate "open innovation" programs harvest grassroots ingenuity while maintaining intellectual property control. Government participatory budgeting often limits citizen input to marginal budgetary decisions while preserving structural inequality.
  3. Repression targets initiatives that resist absorption through surveillance, legal harassment, or violence. The criminalization of mutual aid during crises (as seen in some pandemic responses) or the suppression of digital sovereignty tools through anti-encryption laws exemplify this strategy.

Effective mitigation requires institutional firewalls separating emergent organizations from co-optative pressures. Legal safeguards protecting cooperative autonomy, public benefit corporation structures, and community land trusts prevent asset stripping. Funds bypassing private finance—public banking, sovereign wealth mechanisms, community endowment funds—reduce dependency on financial markets. Democratic governance structures with rotating leadership and member control prevent bureaucratic capture. The real movement must defend its own conditions of possibility.

Temporal Sequencing and Scenarios: Concrete Illustrations

Organizational take-off unfolds through material conditions rather than predetermined timelines. Three scenarios illustrate the range of possibility with specific indicators:

1. Optimistic Scenario: Crisis Threshold Triggering Rapid Transformation (5–15 year horizon)
Cascading climate disasters, financial collapse, or geopolitical rupture create conditions where existing institutions cannot deliver, and emergent organizations demonstrate superior functionality. Political coalitions crystallize around immediate needs, and international solidarity provides resources. Indicators: Municipalist networks in Southern Europe (Barcelona, Naples) link with solidarity economy movements in Latin America (Brazil's MST, Argentine recuperated factories), creating transnational infrastructure for needs-based production. Digital commons achieve critical mass, providing alternative communication and coordination platforms.

2. Middling Scenario: Gradual Accumulation Without Decisive Rupture (20–40 year horizon)
Capabilities develop through defensive adaptation, but social relations transform slowly, constrained by institutional inertia. Organizational take-off occurs sectorally and regionally, creating archipelagos of alternative coordination within persistent capitalist dominance. Indicators: Platform cooperatives capture significant market share in specific sectors (delivery, creative work). Community energy projects achieve energy sovereignty in particular municipalities. Public banking initiatives spread across regions but face constant regulatory containment. Transformation proceeds through attrition rather than breakthrough.

3. Pessimistic Scenario: Reactionary Capture of Developed Capabilities (Ongoing threat)
The organizational capacity for coordination developed through crisis is channeled toward authoritarian consolidation or sectarian conflict. The interregnum extends indefinitely, with periodic crises generating capabilities that reactionary formations capture more effectively. Historical precedent: The organizational forms developed through early 20th century labor and socialist movements were partly captured by fascist and Stalinist bureaucracies. Contemporary risk: Digital surveillance capacities developed for commercial purposes are repurposed for authoritarian social control. Climate adaptation becomes militarized privilege rather than collective right.

This scenario threatens unless proactive organizational development creates viable alternatives that demonstrate both efficacy and ethical superiority.

VI. Practical Pathways: The Movement in Action

The following pathways translate the real movement into practice. Each emerges from existing material conditions rather than ideal plans, addressing contradictions that have become practically intolerable.

First, pilot Global Care and Climate Corps at municipal scale. These programs link youth employment, care services, and climate adaptation through public employment frameworks. They emerge from the material convergence of unemployment crises, care deficits (Fraser, 2016), and climate adaptation needs. Metrics include jobs created per million invested, service coverage in underserved areas, and organizational learning documented through operational manuals. Successful pilots demonstrate the viability of needs-based coordination. Example: The "Care Municipalism" experiments in Basque country, which integrate eldercare, youth employment, and sustainable agriculture through public-community partnerships.

Second, develop legal templates for municipal cooperative conversions. Private infrastructure, factories, data centers, and utility grids facing closure or sale can convert to cooperative or public trust ownership. These conversions emerge from the material failure of private ownership to maintain essential services. Templates address governance structures ensuring democratic control, financing mechanisms reducing dependency on private capital, and transitional arrangements maintaining continuity. Example: The "Cleveland Model" of worker-cooperative networks anchored by large institutions like hospitals, providing stable demand and technical support.

Third, establish regional knowledge commons for operational procedures. How-to manuals from crisis operations, climate adaptation projects, and cooperative formations should be collected, systematized, and licensed as open public goods. These repositories emerge from the recognition that knowledge developed through struggle is repeatedly lost when specific initiatives dissolve. Commons prevent redundant learning and accelerate organizational development. Example: The "Transition Network's" open-source toolkit for community resilience projects, continuously updated through practitioner input.

Fourth, create institutional firewalls through public banking and sovereign funds. Organizations dependent on private finance face perpetual pressure toward profit maximization. Public financial institutions with explicit social mandates can provide patient capital, reducing vulnerability to market discipline. These institutions emerge from the material failure of private finance to meet social needs. Example: The Public Bank of Amsterdam, designed to fund green transitions and social housing while operating democratically.

Fifth, build transnational solidarity networks linking local initiatives—beyond NGO dependency. Organizational take-off in one context creates resources for others: technical assistance, legal templates, financial solidarity, political protection. These networks must develop asymmetric reciprocity that avoids recreating donor-recipient hierarchies. Models: The "Fearless Cities" network facilitates peer-to-peer learning between municipalist movements. The "International Cooperative Alliance" provides solidarity funds controlled by member organizations rather than external donors. Digital platforms like "Commonfare" enable direct resource sharing between communities without bureaucratic intermediaries.

VII. Conclusion: The Abolition of the Present State

The Hegelian-Marxist conception of self-consciousness as collective know-how reframes prospects for transformation. The question is not whether individuals have achieved correct consciousness but whether capabilities developed through struggle can be organized through social relations enabling the abolition of existing conditions. Self-consciousness is the achievement of capacity for self-determination—collective capacity to direct social production toward human need rather than profit (Marx, 1844; Hegel, 1807).

This self-consciousness develops through practice, not theory. It is partial, shaped by alienation and domination that characterize conditions of emergence. And it is dynamic, developing through contradiction between capabilities practice creates and social relations organizing them. The real movement does not proceed from an ideal blueprint but from the material necessity of addressing conditions that have become intolerable.

The interregnum intensifies this contradiction. Old orders decompose; new orders cannot yet be born. In this gap, the question of self-consciousness becomes decisive: whether know-how developed through defensive adaptation can achieve organizational take-off, whether collective capacity for coordination can be directed toward the abolition of capitalist constraints.

The prospects depend on specific organizational forms through which developed capabilities achieve collective self-consciousness and political direction. The task is building organizations that develop consciousness through coordination around immediate needs, demonstrating alternative viability, accumulating procedural knowledge enabling different social relations. Communism is not an ideal to which reality must conform but the real movement which abolishes the present state of things (Marx & Engels, 1845–1846). Self-consciousness, in the end, is not what precedes this movement but what emerges from it: collective capacity for self-determination that constitutes genuine freedom.
